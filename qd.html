Quantum Dots: Nanoscale Semiconductor Particles

Quantum dots (QDs) are nanometer-scale semiconductor particles that exhibit unique optical and electronic properties due to quantum confinement effects. Their size-tunable bandgap allows precise control over their light emission and absorption characteristics.

Key Properties

Size-Dependent Emission

Quantum dots emit light at specific wavelengths determined by their size. Smaller dots emit higher energy (bluer) light, while larger dots emit lower energy (redder) light.

High Quantum Yield

Quantum dots can convert up to 100% of absorbed photons into emitted photons, making them extremely bright fluorophores.

Narrow Emission Spectra

QDs typically have emission bandwidths of 20-30 nm, much narrower than organic dyes (50-100 nm), enabling better spectral resolution.

Photostability

Quantum dots are highly resistant to photobleaching, maintaining their brightness much longer than organic fluorophores.

Common Materials

Material Emission Range Applications
CdSe (Cadmium Selenide) 500-650 nm (visible) Displays, biological labeling
PbS (Lead Sulfide) 800-2000 nm (NIR) Photodetectors, solar cells
InP (Indium Phosphide) 550-750 nm (visible-red) Cadmium-free alternatives
Perovskite QDs 400-700 nm (tunable) LEDs, lasers, photovoltaics

Applications

  • Display Technology: QLED TVs with superior color purity and brightness
  • Biological Imaging: Multicolor cellular labeling and tracking
  • Solar Cells: Enhanced light absorption in photovoltaics
  • Quantum Computing: As qubits in solid-state quantum computers
  • Security Inks: Anti-counterfeiting measures using invisible QD patterns
